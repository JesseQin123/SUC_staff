# Solo Unicorn Events and Content Playbook

## 1. Event Purpose

Events are not isolated networking moments. Each event should deepen trust, expose real work, produce specific feedback, create a useful match or project, and generate reusable institutional knowledge.

## 2. Core Formats

### Founder Salon

Small, high-context discussion among founders and operators. Outcome: one sharper question, one useful connection, and one documented insight per participant.

### Round Table

Structured peer exchange around a defined operating problem. Outcome: contrasting cases, decision criteria, and a short Field Note.

### Workflow Critique

A member shows an actual workflow, system, or agent. Participants diagnose bottlenecks and propose measurable improvements. Outcome: before/after workflow, owner, and next experiment.

### Hackathon

Time-boxed collaborative building. Outcome: working prototype, evidence, retrospective, and an explicit decision to continue, pivot, archive, or publish.

## 3. Event Production SOP

### Six to four weeks before

- Define audience, problem, desired outcome, and approval owner.
- Select format and proposed date.
- Prepare guest and venue shortlist.
- Identify privacy, recording, sponsor, or partner implications.

### Three to two weeks before

- Obtain human approval for guest commitments and public description.
- Create registration page and capacity policy.
- Send invitations to approved audiences.
- Prepare run of show, moderator brief, participant prompts, and accessibility notes.

### One week before

- Confirm attendance and waiting list.
- Share expectations: real work, specific feedback, confidentiality, and consent.
- Confirm recording/photo policy and collect explicit consent where applicable.
- Prepare attendee context for the human host using minimum necessary data.

### Event day

- Check in participants and repeat behavioral/recording rules.
- Capture decisions, questions, experiments, and follow-ups rather than a verbatim private transcript by default.
- Route safety or conduct issues to the human host.

### Within 48 hours

- Send thank-you and approved follow-ups.
- Produce an internal retrospective: attendance, no-show, useful outcomes, issues, and next actions.
- Draft Field Notes without publishing private details.
- Create owner and due date for every promised action.

## 4. Content Pipeline

`Source → Permission check → Draft → Fact check → Human approval → Publish → Archive → Measure`

Sources include event notes, member cases, project retrospectives, interviews, research results, and operating data.

Every draft should identify:

- Intended audience and channel.
- Source materials and factual claims.
- Member/partner names and permission status.
- Facts that require confirmation.
- Suggested title, summary, call to action, and reuse formats.

## 5. Field Notes Standard

A strong Field Note contains:

1. The real operating problem.
2. Context and constraints.
3. What was tried.
4. What worked and failed.
5. Evidence and limitations.
6. A reusable checklist, template, or decision rule.
7. The next experiment.

Do not turn private discussion into public content merely because it is interesting. Remove or generalize identities unless explicit approval was recorded.

## 6. Multilingual Content

English, Chinese, and Japanese versions should preserve meaning, institutional positioning, and approval boundaries. Translation must not strengthen claims, invent affiliations, or convert exploratory language into a commitment.

Japanese partner materials should clearly label items as proposed, invited, under discussion, approved, or completed.
